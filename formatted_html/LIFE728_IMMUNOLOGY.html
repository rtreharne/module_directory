Blurb
Overview
This module serves as a key subject area for students taking the MSc Infection and Immunity Programme and might also be taken by other MSc, MBioSci and MRes students whose interests include infection and immunology. The module is particularly relevant in the context of the pandemic and the future skills need. The module introduces students to the immune responses to infection through research-connected teaching. The content conveys immune responses that are relevant to microbial diseases, including those caused by bacteria, viruses and parasites.
This research-connected module comprises lectures, seminars and workshops incorporating active learning approaches that will support students to synthesise and evaluate relative merits, attributes, issues and applications of current and core topics to increase student confidence. There will be 14 hours of lectures and 8 hours of workshops plus 4 hours of seminars. Assessment will include: graphic illustration of interaction scenarios using online resources to present the extent of host immune and pathogen interactions; and poster presentation of key immune responses developed from workshops and student-led discussions relevant to a variety of presented topics.
Materials will be included on the VLE to assist and promote assimilation and appraisal of the module content and together with online graphics tools will increase digital fluency.
Educational Aims
To develop principal skills in presenting immunological interactions and responses using graphics, posters and verbal communication that are transferable and key for employability.

To aid critical evaluation of current topics, problems and developments in immunological research through advanced scholarship

To evaluate methodologies and develop critiques of approaches used for interrogating the host immune response and integrating functions and evasion mechanisms of pathogens
Marketing Module Synopsis
This is a key module for students on the MSc Infection and Immunity Programme and might also be taken by other MSc, MBioSci and MRes students whose interests include infection and immunology. The module is topical in light of the pandemic and will address areas of research-connected immunology teaching across areas of broad relevance to infectious disease, as well as to coronavirus. The module includes research connected lectures, workshops and structured discussions on selected texts as student-led seminars. The content will focus on areas of immunology that support the programme and are relevant in that: (i) they are current/topical or (ii) they address fundamental questions of general importance. In addition, students will also be supported with key research and ideas in immune mechanisms and host defence, showing how research fields progress and our understanding of defence mechanisms develop. The module assessments are aimed at: 1) using graphics to show illustratively specific and complex immunological host-pathogen interactions and will aid skills in image design; and 2) poster presentation to demonstrate immunological interactions based on the student-led workshops on host defence and microbial evasion.
Outline Syllabus
Lectures:
The module will provide a framework of lectures on host immunity with focus upon the innate and adaptive immune systems and their interplay that support the key immune effectors and there will be particular focus on phagocytes, dendritic cells, and lymphoid tissue. To support the dynamic nature of the immune system responses there will be a suite of lectures that convey the evolved responses of key pathogens. This dual focus on pathogen and response will enable students to synthesise and critically evaluate the diversity of approaches to circumventing the immune response and how these pathogens are also tools to unlock understanding the complexity of host immunity by virtue of their ability to overcome barriers.
There are 5 key thematic areas for the module and for the lectures:
Mucosal immunology – with focus on the respiratory tract and the gastrointestinal tract and their responses
Viral pathogens – with emphasis on HIV and coronavirus as two exemplar viruses and their mechanisms for immune bypass
Bacterial pathogens – with emphasis on pneumococcal immunity and microbial evolution of immune evasion determinants
Parasites – focus will be on key stages of life-cycles relevant to host-parasite interactions and immune escape
Vaccine immunology – basis, responses and memory. Focus on pneumococcus vaccine

Workshops: Students will be provided with papers after supporting lectures that will structure discussion in group sessions:
i – Microbiota interactions with immune responses
The workshop will explore the interactions between the gastrointestinal microbiota and the immune system in the intestinal tract, and effect on intestinal homeostasis and inflammation.
ii – Mucosal immune responses to respiratory tract pathogens and vaccines
The workshop will enable students to appraise the immunological and cellular mechanisms at the mucosal level against pathogens in the respiratory tract and how mucosal vaccines work against disease and promoting herd immunity.
iii - Disease models and the path to clinic
The session will direct students to synthesise and understand how vaccines against infectious diseases are evaluated immunologically including the preclinical models and clinical trials before application in population.
iv – Immune evasion by key human pathogens
The session will allow students to develop perspectives and understanding on the common immune evasion mechanisms by many human pathogens, using selected viral and bacterial pathogens to highlight diverse mechanisms.
Seminars:
Student led group contribution to critically evaluate specific topics and breaking research. Students will research the topic in a small group, then individually report to the wider group as seminars. Key topics to be included are i) – How do cytokines contribute to immunity against infection; ii) – How does innate immunity interact with adaptive immune cells for effective responses, and how can we use this knowledge to develop disease-prevention strategy?
Teaching and Learning Methods
Research-connected teaching supported from the current research interests of the University staff will feature as part of the Liverpool hallmarks of curriculum 2021. Research-connected teaching is embedded in all aspects of teaching by experts in their field. Lectures will provide immunological content via key aspects of host systems and to provide the background and supportive context to structure the module and ensure students learn core elements. Digital fluency is practiced by using online platforms. . Group and student-led discussion will be part of the workshops fostering critical thinking. The workshops will provide a platform for students to synthesise and discuss content in greater depth, explore greater breadth of issues and evaluate scenarios and alternative perspectives. Integration of knowledge and content will emerge from discussion of infections and immune response, their diversity and common themes. Seminars will direct student learning of supplied research papers that will be used for student-led discussions and will enable incorporation of research that is current, breaking or major advances. Attendances will be recorded.

Authentic assessment will be used including the use of graphics resources to communicate via storyboarding biological interactions. Graphics will be supported by writing legends and narratives; this teaching and assessment has active learning as its basis.
Self-directed learning will include reading research papers and additional content provided alongside lectures, preparation for workshops and both assimilating and integrating content from across the module; student time will also be spent developing materials and skills prior to assessments.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two coursework assessments. The assessments are designed to be authentic to the research experience.
The first assessment involves graphic illustration of immune interactions using online resources (e.g. Biorender) to present the extent of host immune and pathogen interactions. This assessment is designed to aid presentation skills to communicate biological processes through images needed for posters and presentations for conferences, meetings and interviews.
This assessment will contribute to 40% of the module mark.

The second assessment will be a poster presentation of key immune responses developed from the workshops and student-led discussions and relevant to a variety of presented topics. The poster will be marked with component marks from the design and content and communication from a 5-minute presentation of the poster, with 3 minutes of questions from staff and students. This assessment will contribute to 60% of the module mark.

Reassessment will use the same format but the poster topic and graphic illustration subject submitted will differ. Mark will be capped at 50%. This will take place in the normal reassessement window. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission.Resit opportunity will be possible

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given

Blurb
Overview
This module serves as a key subject area for students taking the MSc Infection and Immunity Programme and might also be taken by other MSc, MBioSci and MRes students whose interests include infection and immunology. The module is particularly relevant in the context of the pandemic and the future skills need. The module introduces students to the immune responses to infection through research-connected teaching. The content conveys immune responses that are relevant to microbial diseases, including those caused by bacteria, viruses and parasites.
This research-connected module comprises lectures, seminars and workshops incorporating active learning approaches that will support students to synthesise and evaluate relative merits, attributes, issues and applications of current and core topics to increase student confidence. There will be 14 hours of lectures and 8 hours of workshops plus 4 hours of seminars. Assessment will include: graphic illustration of interaction scenarios using online resources to present the extent of host immune and pathogen interactions; and poster presentation of key immune responses developed from workshops and student-led discussions relevant to a variety of presented topics.
Materials will be included on the VLE to assist and promote assimilation and appraisal of the module content and together with online graphics tools will increase digital fluency.
Educational Aims
To develop principal skills in presenting immunological interactions and responses using graphics, posters and verbal communication that are transferable and key for employability.

To aid critical evaluation of current topics, problems and developments in immunological research through advanced scholarship

To evaluate methodologies and develop critiques of approaches used for interrogating the host immune response and integrating functions and evasion mechanisms of pathogens
Marketing Module Synopsis
This is a key module for students on the MSc Infection and Immunity Programme and might also be taken by other MSc, MBioSci and MRes students whose interests include infection and immunology. The module is topical in light of the pandemic and will address areas of research-connected immunology teaching across areas of broad relevance to infectious disease, as well as to coronavirus. The module includes research connected lectures, workshops and structured discussions on selected texts as student-led seminars. The content will focus on areas of immunology that support the programme and are relevant in that: (i) they are current/topical or (ii) they address fundamental questions of general importance. In addition, students will also be supported with key research and ideas in immune mechanisms and host defence, showing how research fields progress and our understanding of defence mechanisms develop. The module assessments are aimed at: 1) using graphics to show illustratively specific and complex immunological host-pathogen interactions and will aid skills in image design; and 2) poster presentation to demonstrate immunological interactions based on the student-led workshops on host defence and microbial evasion.
Outline Syllabus
Lectures:
The module will provide a framework of lectures on host immunity with focus upon the innate and adaptive immune systems and their interplay that support the key immune effectors and there will be particular focus on phagocytes, dendritic cells, and lymphoid tissue. To support the dynamic nature of the immune system responses there will be a suite of lectures that convey the evolved responses of key pathogens. This dual focus on pathogen and response will enable students to synthesise and critically evaluate the diversity of approaches to circumventing the immune response and how these pathogens are also tools to unlock understanding the complexity of host immunity by virtue of their ability to overcome barriers.
There are 5 key thematic areas for the module and for the lectures:
Mucosal immunology – with focus on the respiratory tract and the gastrointestinal tract and their responses
Viral pathogens – with emphasis on HIV and coronavirus as two exemplar viruses and their mechanisms for immune bypass
Bacterial pathogens – with emphasis on pneumococcal immunity and microbial evolution of immune evasion determinants
Parasites – focus will be on key stages of life-cycles relevant to host-parasite interactions and immune escape
Vaccine immunology – basis, responses and memory. Focus on pneumococcus vaccine

Workshops: Students will be provided with papers after supporting lectures that will structure discussion in group sessions:
i – Microbiota interactions with immune responses
The workshop will explore the interactions between the gastrointestinal microbiota and the immune system in the intestinal tract, and effect on intestinal homeostasis and inflammation.
ii – Mucosal immune responses to respiratory tract pathogens and vaccines
The workshop will enable students to appraise the immunological and cellular mechanisms at the mucosal level against pathogens in the respiratory tract and how mucosal vaccines work against disease and promoting herd immunity.
iii - Disease models and the path to clinic
The session will direct students to synthesise and understand how vaccines against infectious diseases are evaluated immunologically including the preclinical models and clinical trials before application in population.
iv – Immune evasion by key human pathogens
The session will allow students to develop perspectives and understanding on the common immune evasion mechanisms by many human pathogens, using selected viral and bacterial pathogens to highlight diverse mechanisms.
Seminars:
Student led group contribution to critically evaluate specific topics and breaking research. Students will research the topic in a small group, then individually report to the wider group as seminars. Key topics to be included are i) – How do cytokines contribute to immunity against infection; ii) – How does innate immunity interact with adaptive immune cells for effective responses, and how can we use this knowledge to develop disease-prevention strategy?
Teaching and Learning Methods
Research-connected teaching supported from the current research interests of the University staff will feature as part of the Liverpool hallmarks of curriculum 2021. Research-connected teaching is embedded in all aspects of teaching by experts in their field. Lectures will provide immunological content via key aspects of host systems and to provide the background and supportive context to structure the module and ensure students learn core elements. Digital fluency is practiced by using online platforms. . Group and student-led discussion will be part of the workshops fostering critical thinking. The workshops will provide a platform for students to synthesise and discuss content in greater depth, explore greater breadth of issues and evaluate scenarios and alternative perspectives. Integration of knowledge and content will emerge from discussion of infections and immune response, their diversity and common themes. Seminars will direct student learning of supplied research papers that will be used for student-led discussions and will enable incorporation of research that is current, breaking or major advances. Attendances will be recorded.

Authentic assessment will be used including the use of graphics resources to communicate via storyboarding biological interactions. Graphics will be supported by writing legends and narratives; this teaching and assessment has active learning as its basis.
Self-directed learning will include reading research papers and additional content provided alongside lectures, preparation for workshops and both assimilating and integrating content from across the module; student time will also be spent developing materials and skills prior to assessments.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two coursework assessments. The assessments are designed to be authentic to the research experience.
The first assessment involves graphic illustration of immune interactions using online resources (e.g. Biorender) to present the extent of host immune and pathogen interactions. This assessment is designed to aid presentation skills to communicate biological processes through images needed for posters and presentations for conferences, meetings and interviews.
This assessment will contribute to 40% of the module mark.

The second assessment will be a poster presentation of key immune responses developed from the workshops and student-led discussions and relevant to a variety of presented topics. The poster will be marked with component marks from the design and content and communication from a 5-minute presentation of the poster, with 3 minutes of questions from staff and students. This assessment will contribute to 60% of the module mark.

Reassessment will use the same format but the poster topic and graphic illustration subject submitted will differ. Mark will be capped at 50%. This will take place in the normal reassessement window. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission.Resit opportunity will be possible

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given
