Blurb
Overview
This module introduces students to the biological concepts of diagnostics, intervention and prevention of infectious disease, relevant to current and emerging threats and with perspective on the current pandemic. The module introduces students to key concepts, technologies and multifactorial considerations circumscribing diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines. The research-connected teaching will focus on prevention, limitation and treatment of infectious disease. Content will highlight global citizenship via the interconnected challenges of infection.

The module comprises lectures, workshops and seminars as delivery methods and incorporates active-learning to ensure students can synthesise and evaluate relative merits, attributes, issues and applications of the topics. Students will participate in and receive research-connected teaching across topics that are topical. The module will provide feedback to students on their progress and allow for adjustment of their learning. There are two assessments in the module: 1) an individual extended written report following workshops and student-led discussions with students focussing on a disease and diagnostic, treatment or prevention of infection and relating to the taught content; 2) a student presentation focused on a for/against debate. Materials will be included on the VLE to develop digital fluency and promote assimilation and appraisal of the module content.
Educational Aims
To develop core skills in critically evaluating design and implementation of diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines using written reports generated from appraising student-led discussion workshops and verbal communication, which are transferable and key for employability.

To enhance critical evaluation of the considerations for product development through advanced scholarship of issues, including: industrial partnering, regulatory affairs, testing, governmental support and societal influences

To enable appraisal of established techniques of research and enquiry to determine host contribution and response to interventions for the development of both diagnostic and control strategies
Marketing Module Synopsis
For students with interests in infection biology, the module will enhance skills and enable critically evaluation of key concepts, technologies and multifactorial considerations circumscribing diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines. The module is topical in light of the current pandemic. The research-connected teaching will focus on prevention, limitation and treatment of infectious disease.
The module comprises lectures, workshops and seminars and uses active-learning delivery methods to ensure students can synthesise and evaluate relative merits, attributes, issues and applications of the topics. There are two coursework assessments in the module: 1) writing a report on a selected emerging pathogen that communicates the multifactorial considerations for researchers and society 2) presentation of a key factor that contributes to or affects disease emergence and the response by society or an agency. Materials will be included on the VLE to develop digital fluency and promote assimilation and appraisal of the module content.
Outline Syllabus
Lectures:
Lectures covering subject-specific content areas will showcase different diseases:
Development of new approaches to curb hepatitis B and C viruses : New diagnostic markers to characterise HIV-1 cure and vaccine strategies; Developing Biological Markers to diagnose disease prognosis; New and improved assays for tuberculosis diagnostics; HIV-1 therapeutics from single drug to PrEP; Novel strategies to prevent HIV-1 transmission; Novel drugs to treat tuberculosis; Novel products to control bacterial infections; The history of failed vaccine trials; Novel animal models aiding in the vaccine development of respiratory bacteria; Development of bacterial vaccines for use in resource limited settings; Development of novel therapeutics and vaccine against animal infections in Africa.
Workshops:
Workshops will focus on key themes of:
1) Development of biological markers for use as diagnostic markers for numerous viral diseases
2) Novel approaches to controlling bacterial infections
3) Diagnostics and therapies for TB
4) Advancing respiratory vaccines for utilisation in resource limited settings
5) Interventions for veterinary infectious diseases in Africa and implications for ‘one health’
Seminars:
The seminars will be presented by experts in areas that can provide a perspective of the wider socioeconomic and geopolitical issues surrounding drugs and vaccines with relevance to past and present pandemics and will involve student discussion. This will aid the breadth of understanding and enable student to evaluate and synthesise perspectives that will be included in the written assessment
• Industrial partnering
• Governmental support in public health and strategy development
• Regulatory affairs
• Advocacy work
• NGO groups supporting product development
Teaching and Learning Methods
Research-connected teaching supported from the current research interests of University staff as experts in their field will feature as part of the Liverpool hallmarks of Curriculum 2021 and Strategy 2026. Lectures will cover advanced biological concepts of diagnostics, intervention and prevention of infectious disease, relevant to current and emerging threats and with perspective on the current pandemic.

The module introduces students to key concepts, technologies and multifactorial considerations circumscribing diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines with focus on prevention, limitation and treatment of infectious disease. Content will highlight global citizenship via the interconnected challenges of infection. Digital fluency is practiced by using online platforms. Active-learning through workshops and student-led discussions will provide a platform for students to synthesise and critically evaluate issues in greater depth and explore scenarios and alternative perspectives. Seminars in the module will provide information on the socioeconomic and wider societal issues that affect drugs, therapeutics and vaccines, enabling students to gain a comprehensive understanding. Materials and content will be provided on the VLE and will improve digital fluency. Attendances will be recorded. Authentic assessment styles are included in the module. Self-directed learning will include reading research papers and additional content provided alongside lectures, preparation for workshops and both assimilating and integrating content from across the module; student time will also be spent developing materials and skills prior to assessments.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two coursework assessments. The assessments are designed to be authentic to the research experience. The first assessment is an extended written report of the multifactorial considerations of stakeholders and different strategies for disease interventions and the pathways to implementation. Through participation in seminars, and workshops that will include student-led discussions, students will be guided to focus on particular combined research and stakeholder areas; they will compile a comprehensive report that highlights the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches and considerations. This will require reading and critical evaluation. The reports will provide conclusions and recommendations. This assessment will contribute 60% of the mark.
The second assessment is a presentation in the form of a debate where students will offer for and against arguments relevant to design and development of disease interventions. Each student will be provided with a specific topic during the module (with a for or against claim) and they will be guided to develop a 10 min debate style presentation with slides followed by 5 min of questions and discussion by the students and lecturers in the audience. To ensure this is authentic, votes will be taken prior to the debates and after to see if views have changed. Marking will use a standard SoLS level 7 presentation rubric. The topics for the assessment will be checked for their equity based on similar content and resources being available for fairness. This assessment will contribute 40% of the mark.

All assessments will be provided with feedback. Pass mark for all assessments is 50%. Due to the nature of the second assessment, this is a non-anonymous assessment. The written report is an anonymous assessment. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission of all assessments.

Reassessment strategy: Resit questions will have the same format as the original assessment but will focus on a different topic on the syllabus and will take place in the reassessment window and will be capped at 50%. Resits for the first assessment will be graded by data analysis and writing a small report.

Learning materials will be available to students via the University Virtual Learning environment.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

Overview
This module introduces students to the biological concepts of diagnostics, intervention and prevention of infectious disease, relevant to current and emerging threats and with perspective on the current pandemic. The module introduces students to key concepts, technologies and multifactorial considerations circumscribing diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines. The research-connected teaching will focus on prevention, limitation and treatment of infectious disease. Content will highlight global citizenship via the interconnected challenges of infection.

The module comprises lectures, workshops and seminars as delivery methods and incorporates active-learning to ensure students can synthesise and evaluate relative merits, attributes, issues and applications of the topics. Students will participate in and receive research-connected teaching across topics that are topical. The module will provide feedback to students on their progress and allow for adjustment of their learning. There are two assessments in the module: 1) an individual extended written report following workshops and student-led discussions with students focussing on a disease and diagnostic, treatment or prevention of infection and relating to the taught content; 2) a student presentation focused on a for/against debate. Materials will be included on the VLE to develop digital fluency and promote assimilation and appraisal of the module content.
Educational Aims
To develop core skills in critically evaluating design and implementation of diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines using written reports generated from appraising student-led discussion workshops and verbal communication, which are transferable and key for employability.

To enhance critical evaluation of the considerations for product development through advanced scholarship of issues, including: industrial partnering, regulatory affairs, testing, governmental support and societal influences

To enable appraisal of established techniques of research and enquiry to determine host contribution and response to interventions for the development of both diagnostic and control strategies
Marketing Module Synopsis
For students with interests in infection biology, the module will enhance skills and enable critically evaluation of key concepts, technologies and multifactorial considerations circumscribing diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines. The module is topical in light of the current pandemic. The research-connected teaching will focus on prevention, limitation and treatment of infectious disease.
The module comprises lectures, workshops and seminars and uses active-learning delivery methods to ensure students can synthesise and evaluate relative merits, attributes, issues and applications of the topics. There are two coursework assessments in the module: 1) writing a report on a selected emerging pathogen that communicates the multifactorial considerations for researchers and society 2) presentation of a key factor that contributes to or affects disease emergence and the response by society or an agency. Materials will be included on the VLE to develop digital fluency and promote assimilation and appraisal of the module content.
Outline Syllabus
Lectures:
Lectures covering subject-specific content areas will showcase different diseases:
Development of new approaches to curb hepatitis B and C viruses : New diagnostic markers to characterise HIV-1 cure and vaccine strategies; Developing Biological Markers to diagnose disease prognosis; New and improved assays for tuberculosis diagnostics; HIV-1 therapeutics from single drug to PrEP; Novel strategies to prevent HIV-1 transmission; Novel drugs to treat tuberculosis; Novel products to control bacterial infections; The history of failed vaccine trials; Novel animal models aiding in the vaccine development of respiratory bacteria; Development of bacterial vaccines for use in resource limited settings; Development of novel therapeutics and vaccine against animal infections in Africa.
Workshops:
Workshops will focus on key themes of:
1) Development of biological markers for use as diagnostic markers for numerous viral diseases
2) Novel approaches to controlling bacterial infections
3) Diagnostics and therapies for TB
4) Advancing respiratory vaccines for utilisation in resource limited settings
5) Interventions for veterinary infectious diseases in Africa and implications for ‘one health’
Seminars:
The seminars will be presented by experts in areas that can provide a perspective of the wider socioeconomic and geopolitical issues surrounding drugs and vaccines with relevance to past and present pandemics and will involve student discussion. This will aid the breadth of understanding and enable student to evaluate and synthesise perspectives that will be included in the written assessment
• Industrial partnering
• Governmental support in public health and strategy development
• Regulatory affairs
• Advocacy work
• NGO groups supporting product development
Teaching and Learning Methods
Research-connected teaching supported from the current research interests of University staff as experts in their field will feature as part of the Liverpool hallmarks of Curriculum 2021 and Strategy 2026. Lectures will cover advanced biological concepts of diagnostics, intervention and prevention of infectious disease, relevant to current and emerging threats and with perspective on the current pandemic.

The module introduces students to key concepts, technologies and multifactorial considerations circumscribing diagnostics, therapeutics and vaccines with focus on prevention, limitation and treatment of infectious disease. Content will highlight global citizenship via the interconnected challenges of infection. Digital fluency is practiced by using online platforms. Active-learning through workshops and student-led discussions will provide a platform for students to synthesise and critically evaluate issues in greater depth and explore scenarios and alternative perspectives. Seminars in the module will provide information on the socioeconomic and wider societal issues that affect drugs, therapeutics and vaccines, enabling students to gain a comprehensive understanding. Materials and content will be provided on the VLE and will improve digital fluency. Attendances will be recorded. Authentic assessment styles are included in the module. Self-directed learning will include reading research papers and additional content provided alongside lectures, preparation for workshops and both assimilating and integrating content from across the module; student time will also be spent developing materials and skills prior to assessments.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two coursework assessments. The assessments are designed to be authentic to the research experience. The first assessment is an extended written report of the multifactorial considerations of stakeholders and different strategies for disease interventions and the pathways to implementation. Through participation in seminars, and workshops that will include student-led discussions, students will be guided to focus on particular combined research and stakeholder areas; they will compile a comprehensive report that highlights the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches and considerations. This will require reading and critical evaluation. The reports will provide conclusions and recommendations. This assessment will contribute 60% of the mark.
The second assessment is a presentation in the form of a debate where students will offer for and against arguments relevant to design and development of disease interventions. Each student will be provided with a specific topic during the module (with a for or against claim) and they will be guided to develop a 10 min debate style presentation with slides followed by 5 min of questions and discussion by the students and lecturers in the audience. To ensure this is authentic, votes will be taken prior to the debates and after to see if views have changed. Marking will use a standard SoLS level 7 presentation rubric. The topics for the assessment will be checked for their equity based on similar content and resources being available for fairness. This assessment will contribute 40% of the mark.

All assessments will be provided with feedback. Pass mark for all assessments is 50%. Due to the nature of the second assessment, this is a non-anonymous assessment. The written report is an anonymous assessment. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission of all assessments.

Reassessment strategy: Resit questions will have the same format as the original assessment but will focus on a different topic on the syllabus and will take place in the reassessment window and will be capped at 50%.
