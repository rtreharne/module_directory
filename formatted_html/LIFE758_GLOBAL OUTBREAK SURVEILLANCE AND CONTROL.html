Blurb
Overview
This module will develop students’ knowledge, skills, and methods for investigating and controlling global human and animal disease outbreaks using a practical One Health framework. It will provide students with an opportunity to deal with the different phases, complexities, and cross-sectoral workings of outbreaks at global, national, and local levels, from the start to finish [semi-simulated]. The module will comprise 10 main themes/topic areas, delivered over two semesters through a series of lectures from senior academics, seminars [guest lectures] from key expert stakeholders, and interactive workshops to ensure students can process, evaluate, and apply knowledge, skills, and methods of the topics. A diverse range of analytical, written, verbal and visual methods will be used which are transferable and key for employability. Assessments will include i) infographic illustrations of control measures ii) an audio or video presentation of a stakeholder policy or communication document and iii) a written report comparing two national strategies. The module will provide feedback to students on their progress and allow for adjustment of their learning.
Educational Aims
The module aims to:

To develop core skills in presenting and the communication of global outbreaks using graphics, student-led discussion workshops and verbal communication, which are transferable and key for employability

To aid critical evaluation of current topics, problems, and developments in global outbreaks though advanced scholarship of issues, including intersectoral collaboration, public health policy and societal implementation

To evaluate methodologies and develop critiques of cross-disciplinary approaches used for the investigation, surveillance and control of outbreaks at global, national, and local levels
Marketing Module Synopsis
This is a key module for students with an interest in developing knowledge, skills, and techniques for investigating and controlling global human and animal disease outbreaks. The module is topical and timely, in light of the recent pandemic and will provide students with an opportunity to deal with the different phases and complexities of outbreaks at global, national, and local levels, from the start to finish using a variety of relevant exemplars.The module comprises lectures, seminars, and workshops led by leading academics, and external key stakeholders with expertise in detection, control, surveillance, communication, governance, policy, bioinformatics, economic and community engagement applications, all integral to the investigation of, and control of outbreaks. The module uses active-learning delivery methods to ensure students can process and evaluate the topics, and has three assessments aimed at developing knowledge and skills in infographics, verbal communication and written reports.
Outline Syllabus
The module will comprise of 10 main theme/ topic areas related to both human and veterinary disease, delivered over two semesters through a series of lectures from senior academics, seminars [guest lectures] from key expert stakeholders, and interactive student-led workshops.

Semester 1 module composition
This will comprise five key thematic sessions each with specific approaches and learning skills.

Lectures
Standard lectures covering the initial phases of the investigation and control of a global human and animal outbreak. In addition, module introductory and revision lectures are provided.
1. The History: Historical background of global outbreaks - focus on pathogens, populations, partnerships
2. The Event: Defining/Identifying the outbreak event and formulating a hypothesis
3. The Response: Assessing the implementation of different control interventions and strategies across the different disciplines
4. The Stakeholders: Determining key roles and responsibilities of different organisations and personnel
5. The Communication: Writing for official reports, public communication briefs, engaging with the mediaSeminars

Seminars
Guest lectures [with Q&A] by experts in their fields, or visits to their workplace, linked to the themed topic areas.
1. Industry: e.g., Mologic diagnostic development and use, the first Liverpool studies (theme 2)
2. Government Agency: e.g., UKHSA/CDC on procedure for a human intervention scenario (theme 3)
3. Government Agency: e.g., DEFRA / APHA on critical partnerships in an animal outbreak scenario (theme 4)
4. Commercial Media: e.g., Newspaper, Radio station visit or other (theme 5)

Workshops: Workshops include four session that focus on human or animal historical events, interventions, stakeholders, and the media coverage of selected diseases. The workshop tasks require students to work individually as well as in small groups, to develop, discuss and/or debate topics, thus providing a mixed method interactive learning approach.
1: Paper: Review, discuss and present epidemiological aspects of historical zoonotic outbreaks
2: Debate. Review and debate the pros/cons of different interventions for a human outbreak
3. Draw: Develop and present a stakeholder map ‘illustration’, highlighting key roles (human/animal)
4. Media: Compare media coverage and modes of communication of an outbreak (human/animal) with actual data in journal / database


Semester 2 module composition
This comprises five key thematic sessions each with specific approaches and learning skills.

Lectures: Standard lectures covering the post-initial phases of the investigation and control of a global human and animal outbreak. In addition, module introductory and revision lectures are provided.
1. The Epidemiology: Implementing follow-up studies to test hypotheses, risk factors, control interventions
2. The Surveillance: Assessing accessibility and utility of global, national, and local systems, data and strategies
3. The Data: Understanding the scope and role of health informatics, bioinformatics, and biobank data
4. The Economics: Determining the cost and impact of control interventions and strategies
5. The Society: Assessing the behavioural/social impact on communities

Seminars: Guest lectures [with Q&A] by experts in their fields, or visits to their workplace, linked to the themed topic areas.
1. Industry: e.g., Pharma – vaccine development, trials, and impact, visit to AstraZeneca (theme 1)
2. Surveillance: e.g., Organisation responsible for surveillance systems e.g., Test and trace (theme 2)
3. Biobank: e.g., Visit to hospital/organisation responsible for biobank data (theme 3)
4. Community: Liverpool City Council on Covid response / Liberia MoH on Ebola impact (theme 5)

Workshops: Workshops include four session that focus on human or animal epidemiological studies, surveillance systems, bioinformatics, and economics of selected diseases. The workshop tasks require students to work individually as well as in small groups, to develop, discuss and/or debate topics, thus providing as mixed method interactive learning approach.

1. Visual: identify, develop, and compare visual abstracts on an epidemiological follow-up study
2. Debate. Discuss and debate the pros/cons of different global surveillance systems
3. Paper: Review and discuss the main tools and uses in bioinformatics, and cross-agency working
4. Media: Identify, compare, and discuss media articles/posts on community perceptions and impact
Teaching and Learning Methods
The teaching methods below were designed to align with the Liverpool Curriculum Framework.

Teaching method 1 – Lectures (asynchronous)
Research-connected lectures will introduce key subject matter, drawing upon high-profile real-world examples related to the 10 main theme/ topic areas outlined in the Outline Syllabus section, including in Semester 1: The History (Theme 1), Event (Theme 2), Response (Theme 3), Stakeholders (Theme 4), and Communication (Theme 5) and in Semester 2: The Epidemiology (Theme 6), Surveillance (Theme 7), Data (Theme 8), Economics (Theme 9), and Society (Theme 10). Relevant materials and content will be provided on the University Virtual Learning Environment (Canvas) and will improve digital fluency.
Attendance recorded: Y

Teaching method 2 – Seminars (Guest lectures)
Guest lectures (with Q&A) by experts in their fields, linked to the themed topic areas will direct student learning, allow interactive discussions, and will enable incorporation of research that is current, breaking, or considered a major advance. Relevant materials and content will be provided on the VLE and will improve digital fluency.
Attendance recorded: Y

Teaching method 3 – Workshops
The workshop tasks, linked to themed topic areas, will require students to work individually as well as in small groups, to develop, discuss and/or debate topics, thus providing a mixed method interactive learning approach. The workshops will foster critical thinking and provide a platform for students to synthesise and discuss content in greater depth, explore greater breadth of issues and evaluate scenarios and alternative perspectives.
Attendance recorded: Y

Teaching method 4 – Self-directed learning
Self-directed learning will include reading research papers and additional content provided alongside lectures, preparation for workshops and both assimilating and integrating content from across the module; student time will also be spent developing materials and skills prior to assessments.
Attendance recorded: N/A

Teaching method - Assessment
Authentic assessment styles will be used, including the use of graphics resources to communicate via storyboarding biological interactions. Graphics will be supported by writing legends and narratives; this teaching and assessment has active learning as its basis.
Attendance recorded: N/A


Exceptional circumstances
Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g., global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE. and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
Assessment Strategy
The proposed approaches to assessment (Tasks outlined below) respect the Liverpool Curriculum Framework. In particular, the assessment strategy has been designed to include authentic assessment and active learning, and to develop graduate attributes of digital fluency, and confidence.

The module will be assessed via three tasks, which are designed to provide research and communication experience.

Assessment 1: An infographic illustration (poster) of control measures and intervention strategies used for a human or animal global outbreak. The assessment is designed to aid skills to visually communicate key aspects that may help to prevent and mitigate infection among at-risk populations.

Assessment 2: The second assessment will be a video of a stakeholder policy or communication report or briefing on a human or animal outbreak (e.g., government). The 5-min video will be in the broadcast style of aTV or video clip. The assessment is designed to aid skills in verbal and visual communication of factual data succinctly.

Assessment 3: The final assessment will be a written report on national intervention and surveillance strategies. The students will be required to critically review and compare two countries strategies, highlighting the similarities and differences and the impact that they had on the control of an outbreak.

Reassessment
For all assessments, there is a resit opportunity. Students are essentially revising the original piece of work in any resit assessments. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission. This is an anonymous assessment. Reassessment will take place in the normal reassessment period and capped at 50%.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the University Virtual Learning environment (Canvas) and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

This will comprise five key thematic sessions each with specific approaches and learning skills.

Lectures
Standard lectures covering the initial phases of the investigation and control of a global human and animal outbreak. In addition, module introductory and revision lectures are provided.
1. The History: Historical background of global outbreaks - focus on pathogens, populations, partnerships
2. The Event: Defining/Identifying the outbreak event and formulating a hypothesis
3. The Response: Assessing the implementation of different control interventions and strategies across the different disciplines
4. The Stakeholders: Determining key roles and responsibilities of different organisations and personnel
5. The Communication: Writing for official reports, public communication briefs, engaging with the mediaSeminars

Seminars
Guest lectures [with Q&A] by experts in their fields, or visits to their workplace, linked to the themed topic areas.
1. Industry: e.g., Mologic diagnostic development and use, the first Liverpool studies (theme 2)
2. Government Agency: e.g., UKHSA/CDC on procedure for a human intervention scenario (theme 3)
3. Government Agency: e.g., DEFRA / APHA on critical partnerships in an animal outbreak scenario (theme 4)
4. Commercial Media: e.g., Newspaper, Radio station visit or other (theme 5)

Workshops: Workshops include four session that focus on human or animal historical events, interventions, stakeholders, and the media coverage of selected diseases. The workshop tasks require students to work individually as well as in small groups, to develop, discuss and/or debate topics, thus providing a mixed method interactive learning approach.
1: Paper: Review, discuss and present epidemiological aspects of historical zoonotic outbreaks
2: Debate. Review and debate the pros/cons of different interventions for a human outbreak
3. Draw: Develop and present a stakeholder map ‘illustration’, highlighting key roles (human/animal)
4. Media: Compare media coverage and modes of communication of an outbreak (human/animal) with actual data in journal / database


Semester 2 module composition
This comprises five key thematic sessions each with specific approaches and learning skills.

Lectures: Standard lectures covering the post-initial phases of the investigation and control of a global human and animal outbreak. In addition, module introductory and revision lectures are provided.
1. The Epidemiology: Implementing follow-up studies to test hypotheses, risk factors, control interventions
2. The Surveillance: Assessing accessibility and utility of global, national, and local systems, data and strategies
3. The Data: Understanding the scope and role of health informatics, bioinformatics, and biobank data
4. The Economics: Determining the cost and impact of control interventions and strategies
5. The Society: Assessing the behavioural/social impact on communities

Seminars: Guest lectures [with Q&A] by experts in their fields, or visits to their workplace, linked to the themed topic areas.
1. Industry: e.g., Pharma – vaccine development, trials, and impact, visit to AstraZeneca (theme 1)
2. Surveillance: e.g., Organisation responsible for surveillance systems e.g., Test and trace (theme 2)
3. Biobank: e.g., Visit to hospital/organisation responsible for biobank data (theme 3)
4. Community: Liverpool City Council on Covid response / Liberia MoH on Ebola impact (theme 5)

Workshops: Workshops include four session that focus on human or animal epidemiological studies, surveillance systems, bioinformatics, and economics of selected diseases. The workshop tasks require students to work individually as well as in small groups, to develop, discuss and/or debate topics, thus providing as mixed method interactive learning approach.

1. Visual: identify, develop, and compare visual abstracts on an epidemiological follow-up study
2. Debate. Discuss and debate the pros/cons of different global surveillance systems
3. Paper: Review and discuss the main tools and uses in bioinformatics, and cross-agency working
4. Media: Identify, compare, and discuss media articles/posts on community perceptions and impact
Teaching and Learning Methods
The teaching methods below were designed to align with the Liverpool Curriculum Framework.

Teaching method 1 – Lectures (asynchronous)
Research-connected lectures will introduce key subject matter, drawing upon high-profile real-world examples related to the 10 main theme/ topic areas outlined in the Outline Syllabus section, including in Semester 1: The History (Theme 1), Event (Theme 2), Response (Theme 3), Stakeholders (Theme 4), and Communication (Theme 5) and in Semester 2: The Epidemiology (Theme 6), Surveillance (Theme 7), Data (Theme 8), Economics (Theme 9), and Society (Theme 10). Relevant materials and content will be provided on the University Virtual Learning Environment (Canvas) and will improve digital fluency.
Attendance recorded: Y

Teaching method 2 – Seminars (Guest lectures)
Guest lectures (with Q&A) by experts in their fields, linked to the themed topic areas will direct student learning, allow interactive discussions, and will enable incorporation of research that is current, breaking, or considered a major advance. Relevant materials and content will be provided on the VLE and will improve digital fluency.
Attendance recorded: Y

Teaching method 3 – Workshops
The workshop tasks, linked to themed topic areas, will require students to work individually as well as in small groups, to develop, discuss and/or debate topics, thus providing a mixed method interactive learning approach. The workshops will foster critical thinking and provide a platform for students to synthesise and discuss content in greater depth, explore greater breadth of issues and evaluate scenarios and alternative perspectives.
Attendance recorded: Y

Teaching method 4 – Self-directed learning
Self-directed learning will include reading research papers and additional content provided alongside lectures, preparation for workshops and both assimilating and integrating content from across the module; student time will also be spent developing materials and skills prior to assessments.
Attendance recorded: N/A

Authentic assessment styles will be used, including the use of graphics resources to communicate via storyboarding biological interactions. Graphics will be supported by writing legends and narratives; this teaching and assessment has active learning as its basis.
Attendance recorded: N/A


Exceptional circumstances
Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g., global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
Assessment Strategy
The proposed approaches to assessment (Tasks outlined below) respect the Liverpool Curriculum Framework. In particular, the assessment strategy has been designed to include authentic assessment and active learning, and to develop graduate attributes of digital fluency, and confidence.

The module will be assessed via three tasks, which are designed to provide research and communication experience.

Assessment 1: An infographic illustration (poster) of control measures and intervention strategies used for a human or animal global outbreak. The assessment is designed to aid skills to visually communicate key aspects that may help to prevent and mitigate infection among at-risk populations.

Assessment 2: The second assessment will be a video of a stakeholder policy or communication report or briefing on a human or animal outbreak (e.g., government). The 5-min video will be in the broadcast style of aTV or video clip. The assessment is designed to aid skills in verbal and visual communication of factual data succinctly.

Assessment 3: The final assessment will be a written report on national intervention and surveillance strategies. The students will be required to critically review and compare two countries strategies, highlighting the similarities and differences and the impact that they had on the control of an outbreak.

Reassessment
For all assessments, there is a resit opportunity. Students are essentially revising the original piece of work in any resit assessments. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission. This is an anonymous assessment. Reassessment will take place in the normal reassessment period and capped at 50%.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the University Virtual Learning environment (Canvas) and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

Overview
This module will develop students’ knowledge, skills, and methods for investigating and controlling global human and animal disease outbreaks using a practical One Health framework. It will provide students with an opportunity to deal with the different phases, complexities, and cross-sectoral workings of outbreaks at global, national, and local levels, from the start to finish [semi-simulated]. The module will comprise 10 main themes/topic areas, delivered over two semesters through a series of lectures from senior academics, seminars [guest lectures] from key expert stakeholders, and interactive workshops to ensure students can process, evaluate, and apply knowledge, skills, and methods of the topics. A diverse range of analytical, written, verbal and visual methods will be used which are transferable and key for employability. Assessments will include i) infographic illustrations of control measures ii) an audio or video presentation of a stakeholder policy or communication document and iii) a written report comparing two national strategies. The module will provide feedback to students on their progress and allow for adjustment of their learning.
Educational Aims
The module aims to:

To develop core skills in presenting and the communication of global outbreaks using graphics, student-led discussion workshops and verbal communication, which are transferable and key for employability

To aid critical evaluation of current topics, problems, and developments in global outbreaks though advanced scholarship of issues, including intersectoral collaboration, public health policy and societal implementation

To evaluate methodologies and develop critiques of cross-disciplinary approaches used for the investigation, surveillance and control of outbreaks at global, national, and local levels

Assessment Strategy
The proposed approaches to assessment (Tasks outlined below) respect the Liverpool Curriculum Framework. In particular, the assessment strategy has been designed to include authentic assessment and active learning, and to develop graduate attributes of digital fluency, and confidence.

The module will be assessed via three tasks, which are designed to provide research and communication experience.

Assessment 1: An infographic illustration (poster) of control measures and intervention strategies used for a human or animal global outbreak. The assessment is designed to aid skills to visually communicate key aspects that may help to prevent and mitigate infection among at-risk populations.

Assessment 2: The second assessment will be a video of a stakeholder policy or communication report or briefing on a human or animal outbreak (e.g., government). The 5-min video will be in the broadcast style of aTV or video clip. The assessment is designed to aid skills in verbal and visual communication of factual data succinctly.

Assessment 3: The final assessment will be a written report on national intervention and surveillance strategies. The students will be required to critically review and compare two countries strategies, highlighting the similarities and differences and the impact that they had on the control of an outbreak.

Reassessment
For all assessments, there is a resit opportunity. Students are essentially revising the original piece of work in any resit assessments. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission. This is an anonymous assessment. Reassessment will take place in the normal reassessment period and capped at 50%.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the University Virtual Learning environment (Canvas) and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

Notes
This is a key module for students with an interest in developing knowledge, skills, and techniques for investigating and controlling global human and animal disease outbreaks. The module is topical and timely, in light of the recent pandemic and will provide students with an opportunity to deal with the different phases and complexities of outbreaks at global, national, and local levels, from the start to finish using a variety of relevant exemplars.
