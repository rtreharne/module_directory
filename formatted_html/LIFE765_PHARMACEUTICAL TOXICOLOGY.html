Blurb
Overview
This is a key module for students on the MSc Pharmacology and Toxicology programme and might also be taken by other MSc, MBiolSci and MRes students interested in core and novel aspects of pharmaceutical toxicology. The module includes research connected lectures and seminars delivered by leading academic researchers based at the University of Liverpool, as well as external speakers from the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies. Topics include major organ toxicities, pharmacogenetics, development of in vitro test systems, nanotoxicology and computational/systems toxicology. The two module assessments are aimed at writing reports on topics covered in the first and second half of the module, respectively. Students will be able to select a topic of choice (with a limit placed on the number of students that can select a given topic, to ensure even distribution) which will be directed and marked by the associated relevant member of the teaching delivery team.
Educational Aims
The goal of this module is to provide students with a comprehensive understanding of core and novel aspects of toxicology in the context of the development of new medicines. The specific aims are to:

Familiarise students with core and novel mechanisms underlying major organ toxicities

Make students aware of the human factors that influence susceptibility to drug toxicities

Equip students with the knowledge and skills to enable a critical appraisal of established and novel approaches for studying drug toxicities
Marketing Module Synopsis
This key module in the MSc Pharmacology and Toxicology programme brings together core and novel aspects of toxicology in the context of the development of new medicines. Topics include major organ toxicities, pharmacogenetics, development of in vitro test systems, nanotoxicology and computational/systems toxicology. This builds on local research strengths (e.g. Centre for Drug Safety Science) and strong links with the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies. The module includes research connected lectures and seminars, from leading academic researchers based at the University of Liverpool, as well as external speakers. The two module assessments are aimed at writing reports on topics covered in the first and second half of the module, respectively.
Outline Syllabus
Lectures will provide students with an understanding of both the mechanisms underlying major organ toxicities and the human factors that influence susceptibility to drug toxicities, as well as an ability to appraise established and novel approaches for studying drug toxicities. Content will build on core aspects of cell death, organ toxicity, pharmacogenetics and pharmaco/toxicokinetics that are likely to have been covered at BSc level. New topics include immunotoxicology, mitochondrial toxicity, new approach methodologies, computational/systems toxicology and biocompatibility.

Seminars, delivered by external experts from the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies, will provide students with a broad appreciation of the real-world relevance and application of the material covered in the lectures, in the context of the development of new medicines. This will include concepts such as safety assessment of small molecule/next-generation drug candidates, as well as pre- and post-marketing regulatory considerations.

Library resources will be accessible through the module reading list. Other key learning resources will be accessible through Canvas. Students will be expected to complete independent reading in preparation for seminars. Students will be expected to identify their own supplementary resources, particularly to support the writing of the two coursework assessments.
Teaching and Learning Methods
There will be 30 hours of scheduled learning time, comprising a combination of lectures and seminars. Lectures will be delivered by University staff with research expertise in specific topics. Seminars will be delivered by external experts from the pharmaceutical industry. In both cases, students may be asked to apply their knowledge during the completion of in-session tasks and discussion of the outcomes. Independent learning time will be used to support the scheduled directed learning, including through independent reading of materials related to each of the lectures or preparation for seminars. Research-connected lectures supported by current research interests of the University staff and other experts in the field will provide students with an excellent background and a good understanding of the state-of-the-art (Research connected teaching; Liverpool Curriculum Framework). To foster critical thinking and active learning, seminars will feature group and student-led discussions. Integration of knowledge and content will emerge from discussion of emerging technologies, the drug development landscape, and regulatory requirements (Active learning; Liverpool Curriculum Framework). Attendance will be recorded. Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance for approval of any such changes.

The lecture content, case studies, reading lists, and discussion between students and staff give the students an opportunity to experience and understand cultures other than their own. This inclusive approach fosters respect and equality amongst the students and improves their global perspective (Global citizenship and Inclusivity, Liverpool Curriculum Framework).
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two summative coursework assessments:

(1) Individual written report on one of the topics covered in the first half of the module. The specific topics will be set by the staff delivering content in the first half of the module, encompassing (LO1 and LO2). This assessment will contribute 50 % of the module mark.

(2) Individual written report on one of the topics covered in the second half of the module. The specific topics will be set by the staff delivering content in the second half of the module, encompassing (LO2 and LO3). This assessment will contribute 50 % of the module mark.

Students will be able to select a topic of choice (with a limit placed on the students that can choose a given topic to ensure even distribution) which will be directed and marked by the associated relevant member of the teaching delivery team. Choice of assessment topic (negotiated assessment) is provided to increase inclusivity according to the principles of Universal Design for Learning. This allows students to demonstrate achievement of the learning outcomes using the best topic for them. (Inclusivity; Liverpool Curriculum Framework). Writing of scientific reports is also a skill that is often required by employers or if students continue into a research career and is therefore authentic (Authentic assessment; Liverpool Curriculum Framework).

Feedback on summative assessments will be provided as part of the marking process, in line with University guidelines. No formative assessments will take place.

Reassessment will use the same format but the report topics will differ. Mark will be capped at 50%. This will take place in the normal reassessment window. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission. Resit opportunity will be possible. Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

Blurb
Overview
This is a key module for students on the MSc Pharmacology and Toxicology programme and might also be taken by other MSc, MBiolSci and MRes students interested in core and novel aspects of pharmaceutical toxicology. The module includes research connected lectures and seminars delivered by leading academic researchers based at the University of Liverpool, as well as external speakers from the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies. Topics include major organ toxicities, pharmacogenetics, development of in vitro test systems, nanotoxicology and computational/systems toxicology. The two module assessments are aimed at writing reports on topics covered in the first and second half of the module, respectively. Students will be able to select a topic of choice (with a limit placed on the number of students that can select a given topic, to ensure even distribution) which will be directed and marked by the associated relevant member of the teaching delivery team.
Educational Aims
The goal of this module is to provide students with a comprehensive understanding of core and novel aspects of toxicology in the context of the development of new medicines. The specific aims are to:

Familiarise students with core and novel mechanisms underlying major organ toxicities

Make students aware of the human factors that influence susceptibility to drug toxicities

Equip students with the knowledge and skills to enable a critical appraisal of established and novel approaches for studying drug toxicities
Marketing Module Synopsis
This key module in the MSc Pharmacology and Toxicology programme brings together core and novel aspects of toxicology in the context of the development of new medicines. Topics include major organ toxicities, pharmacogenetics, development of in vitro test systems, nanotoxicology and computational/systems toxicology. This builds on local research strengths (e.g. Centre for Drug Safety Science) and strong links with the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies. The module includes research connected lectures and seminars, from leading academic researchers based at the University of Liverpool, as well as external speakers. The two module assessments are aimed at writing reports on topics covered in the first and second half of the module, respectively.
Outline Syllabus
Lectures will provide students with an understanding of both the mechanisms underlying major organ toxicities and the human factors that influence susceptibility to drug toxicities, as well as an ability to appraise established and novel approaches for studying drug toxicities. Content will build on core aspects of cell death, organ toxicity, pharmacogenetics and pharmaco/toxicokinetics that are likely to have been covered at BSc level. New topics include immunotoxicology, mitochondrial toxicity, new approach methodologies, computational/systems toxicology and biocompatibility.

Seminars, delivered by external experts from the pharmaceutical industry and regulatory agencies, will provide students with a broad appreciation of the real-world relevance and application of the material covered in the lectures, in the context of the development of new medicines. This will include concepts such as safety assessment of small molecule/next-generation drug candidates, as well as pre- and post-marketing regulatory considerations.

Library resources will be accessible through the module reading list. Other key learning resources will be accessible through Canvas. Students will be expected to complete independent reading in preparation for seminars. Students will be expected to identify their own supplementary resources, particularly to support the writing of the two coursework assessments.
Teaching and Learning Methods
There will be 30 hours of scheduled learning time, comprising a combination of lectures and seminars. Lectures will be delivered by University staff with research expertise in specific topics. Seminars will be delivered by external experts from the pharmaceutical industry. In both cases, students may be asked to apply their knowledge during the completion of in-session tasks and discussion of the outcomes. Independent learning time will be used to support the scheduled directed learning, including through independent reading of materials related to each of the lectures or preparation for seminars. Research-connected lectures supported by current research interests of the University staff and other experts in the field will provide students with an excellent background and a good understanding of the state-of-the-art (Research connected teaching; Liverpool Curriculum Framework). To foster critical thinking and active learning, seminars will feature group and student-led discussions. Integration of knowledge and content will emerge from discussion of emerging technologies, the drug development landscape, and regulatory requirements (Active learning; Liverpool Curriculum Framework). Attendance will be recorded. Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance for approval of any such changes.

The lecture content, case studies, reading lists, and discussion between students and staff give the students an opportunity to experience and understand cultures other than their own. This inclusive approach fosters respect and equality amongst the students and improves their global perspective (Global citizenship and Inclusivity, Liverpool Curriculum Framework).
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two summative coursework assessments:

(1) Individual written report on one of the topics covered in the first half of the module. The specific topics will be set by the staff delivering content in the first half of the module, encompassing (LO1 and LO2). This assessment will contribute 50 % of the module mark.

(2) Individual written report on one of the topics covered in the second half of the module. The specific topics will be set by the staff delivering content in the second half of the module, encompassing (LO2 and LO3). This assessment will contribute 50 % of the module mark.

Students will be able to select a topic of choice (with a limit placed on the students that can choose a given topic to ensure even distribution) which will be directed and marked by the associated relevant member of the teaching delivery team. Choice of assessment topic (negotiated assessment) is provided to increase inclusivity according to the principles of Universal Design for Learning. This allows students to demonstrate achievement of the learning outcomes using the best topic for them. (Inclusivity; Liverpool Curriculum Framework). Writing of scientific reports is also a skill that is often required by employers or if students continue into a research career and is therefore authentic (Authentic assessment; Liverpool Curriculum Framework).

Feedback on summative assessments will be provided as part of the marking process, in line with University guidelines. No formative assessments will take place.

Reassessment will use the same format but the report topics will differ. Mark will be capped at 50%. This will take place in the normal reassessment window. Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission. Resit opportunity will be possible. Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
