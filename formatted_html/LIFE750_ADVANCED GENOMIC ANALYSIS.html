Educational Aims
The aim of this course is to equip students with a broad understanding of genomic data and its analysis, and to develop insight into how these methods can be applied to answer biological and medical questions.
Marketing Module Synopsis
Modern biology and medicine are increasingly making use of complex genomic data sets. As a result, there is increasing demand for graduates who can analyse and interpret these data.

In this module, you will learn the fundamentals of a broad range of genomic analyses. You will learn how and when to apply different genomic technologies, and how to analyse the data-- to understand fundamental biological processes, to reconstruct the history of organisms and to trace disease outbreaks, for example.

You will be taught through a combination of lectures, to give a strong grounding in each topic-- followed by hands on workshops-- where you will gain experience in applying your skills to data analysis. Most topics will be covered in two-week sessions, with a typical week consisting of two-hours of contact time.

To demonstrate your mastery of the topics, you will engage in a set of assessments that mirror real-world applications of your knowledge: a poster presentation (30%) on an advanced topic in genomic analysis, and a synthetic report (70%) that applies the material covered to a novel biological context.

We have developed this module, along with all other modules in this Programme, in consultation with partners from both industry and academia, in order to ensure that graduates have skills that are currently in demand.
Outline Syllabus
Topic 1. Introduction
Introduction on how the module will run and introduction to the teaching and learning environment.

Topic 2. Comparative Genomics
An introduction to comparing genomes between species, including comparing gene content, measuring sequence evolution, and understanding structural change. We will work to understand how to design comparative genomic analyses, how to perform basic analyses, and what can be inferred from the outcomes of these analyses.

Topic 3. Phylogenetic Analyses
An introduction to phylogenetic analyses. We will review methods of tree reconstruction, and understand basic hypothesis testing with trees. These methods will then be applied to practical analysis of reconstructing, e.g., a disease outbreak.

Topic 4. Variation within populations of organisms or cells.
An introduction to population genomics, including how it can be applied to individuals in a natural population and to populations of cells within a tumor. We will learn about resequencing data, types of variants and how to call them, and summaries of genetic variation and what they mean. We will apply this knowledge to examining example data resequencing data sets.

Topic 5. Gene regulation
In this topic, we will learn how transcriptomic and epigenomic data can be used to understand gene expression and regulation. We will learn about different types of transcriptomic and epigenomic data, how to design an experiment to make use of these kinds of data, how to perform basic analyses, and what can be inferred from the outcomes of these analyses.

Topic 6. Metagenomics
Metagenomics is used to analyse communities of organisms, often consisting of a mix of species, such as bacteria in the gut or from environmental samples. For this topic, we will learn how to design a metagenomic experiment, and how to analyse the resulting data.
Teaching and Learning Methods
The module will consist of lectures and hands-on workshops with a typical week consisting of two-hour contact time.
Lectures: Lectures will introduce the module and learning environment and provide an in-depth, research-connected teaching background to selected topics. Lectures will cover applications of methods in research and will discuss these in the context of research publications.

Attendance Recorded: yes

Workshops will provide core learning and active-learning exercises analysing example data sets and and their applications in research.

Attendance Recorded: yes

Notes: Students will engage in smaller groups discussions and research activities on a specific topic related to the module content.

The Student Directed Learning element of the module will involve directed reading, completion of workbooks, accessing online resources, searching the literature and critically appraising articles. This will require adaptability & organisational skills.

Curriculum 2021 and the Liverpool Hallmarks are at the centre of this module. Research-connected teaching is embedded in all aspects of teaching. This module contributes to a Programme which is specifically designed to support students for future training in Life Sciences and based on consultations with employers on what skills they would like graduates to have, promoting the employability of graduates. The teaching approach emphasises active learning and practical exercises, providing students with the opportunity to increase their digital fluency. Finally, student confidence will be developed through taking part in group discussions during workshops.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
Assessment Strategy
The module will be assessed via two assessments which were carefully designed to be authentic, to enhance research-connected teaching and active learning experience.

Assessment 1 will consist of a group work which will include data analysis and presenting it in a form of a poster. This assessment will illustrate an aspect of genomics via analysing and interpreting a data set. The poster is expected to be consistent with the state-of-the-art in bioinformatics research, reflecting the digital fluency developed by the students. Group work is designed to develop confidence in collaborative work, preparing students for a professional environment, and confidence via development of communication skills (C2021). This assessment will contribute to 30% of the module mark. The assessment will be graded by a combination of marks for the group for the quality of the overall work and peer-marking to assess individual contributions.

Assessment 2 will be a written report. This assessment will require a synthesising material covered in the course, and applying it to a novel biological context, allowing the students to demonstrate that they understand how and when to apply the skills learned in the module, particularly demonstrating their digital fluency (C2021), as it pertains to genomic data analysis. This will contribute to 70% of the module mark.

All assessments will be provided with feedback. Pass mark for all assessments is 50%. Due to the nature of the first assessment, this is a non-anomymous assessment. The final report is an anonymous assessment.

Reassessment strategy: Resit questions will have the same format as the original assessment but will focus on a different topic on the syllabus and will take place in the reassessment window and will be capped at 50%. Resits for the first assessment will be graded by data analysis and writing a small report.

Standard UoL penalty applies for late submission of all assessments.

Learning materials will be available to students via the University Virtual Learning environment.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the Virtual Learning Environment and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

The aim of this course is to equip students with a broad understanding of these kinds of data and their analyses, and to develop insight into how these methods can be applied to answer biological and medical questions.

Educational Aims
The aim of this course is to equip students with a broad understanding of genomic data and its analysis, and to develop insight into how these methods can be applied to answer biological and medical questions.
Marketing Module Synopsis
Modern biology and medicine are increasingly making use of complex genomic data sets. As a result, there is increasing demand for graduates who can analyse and interpret these data.

In this module, you will learn the fundamentals of a broad range of genomic analyses. You will learn how and when to apply different genomic technologies, and how to analyse the data-- to understand fundamental biological processes, to reconstruct the history of organisms and to trace disease outbreaks, for example.

You will be taught through a combination of lectures, to give a strong grounding in each topic-- followed by hands on workshops-- where you will gain experience in applying your skills to data analysis. Most topics will be covered in two-week sessions, with a typical week consisting of two-hours of contact time.

To demonstrate your mastery of the topics, you will engage in a set of assessments that mirror real-world applications of your knowledge: a poster presentation (30%) on an advanced topic in genomic analysis, and a synthetic report (70%) that applies the material covered to a novel biological context.

We have developed this module, along with all other modules in this Programme, in consultation with partners from both industry and academia, in order to ensure that graduates have skills that are currently in demand.
Outline Syllabus
Topic 1. Introduction
Introduction on how the module will run and introduction to the teaching and learning environment.

Topic 2. Comparative Genomics
An introduction to comparing genomes between species, including comparing gene content, measuring sequence evolution, and understanding structural change. We will work to understand how to design comparative genomic analyses, how to perform basic analyses, and what can be inferred from the outcomes of these analyses.

Topic 3. Phylogenetic Analyses
An introduction to phylogenetic analyses. We will review methods of tree reconstruction, and understand basic hypothesis testing with trees. These methods will then be applied to practical analysis of reconstructing, e.g., a disease outbreak.

Topic 4. Variation within populations of organisms or cells.
An introduction to population genomics, including how it can be applied to individuals in a natural population and to populations of cells within a tumor. We will learn about resequencing data, types of variants and how to call them, and summaries of genetic variation and what they mean. We will apply this knowledge to examining example data resequencing data sets.

Topic 5. Gene regulation
In this topic, we will learn how transcriptomic and epigenomic data can be used to understand gene expression and regulation. We will learn about different types of transcriptomic and epigenomic data, how to design an experiment to make use of these kinds of data, how to perform basic analyses, and what can be inferred from the outcomes of these analyses.

Topic 6. Metagenomics
Metagenomics is used to analyse communities of organisms, often consisting of a mix of species, such as bacteria in the gut or from environmental samples. For this topic, we will learn how to design a metagenomic experiment, and how to analyse the resulting data.
