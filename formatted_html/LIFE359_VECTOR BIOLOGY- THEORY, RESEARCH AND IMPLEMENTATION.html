Blurb
Overview
This module introduces students to current knowledge and research into insects and other vectors of disease. It has a major focus on vector biology, including life cycle, ecology, behaviour and methods of control. The vectors covered include different mosquito species, sandflies, tsetse flies and snails. The emphasis is on how understanding vector biology through theory and research is critical to the implementation of successful control programmes. There is also teaching on research into novel methods of control, focusing on malaria, dengue, sleeping sickness and schistosomiasis. This module is delivered by hybrid model of teaching and assessment and follows the Liverpool Curriculum Framework principles and attributes. Assessments are given as two open book written pieces.
Educational Aims
​To describe current research into vectors and vector-borne diseases

​To demonstrate how this research answers broad-ranging questions in vector biology and leads to novel vector control strategies
​​
​To develop knowledge and deep understanding in tropical disease biology, and ability to apply, critically evaluate and interpret this knowledge to solve complex problems
Marketing Module Synopsis
This module introduces students to current knowledge and research into insects and other vectors of disease. It has a major focus on vector biology, including life cycle, ecology, behaviour and methods of control. The vectors covered include different mosquito species, sandflies, tsetse flies and snails. The emphasis is on how understanding vector biology through theory and research is critical to the implementation of successful control programmes. There is also teaching on research into novel methods of control, focusing on malaria, dengue, sleeping sickness and schistosomiasis. The module is delivered by formal lectures and a revision tutorial, and is assessed by coursework.
Outline Syllabus
The course is comprised of 18 lectures and associated active learning sessions that introduce the field of vector biology and disease transmission with an emphasis on vector control. It is provided by lecturers that are all research active and give a perspective on how research into vector biology is used to inform disease control strategies.

The major topics that are covered in the course include:

Introduction to Insects and arthropods of medical importance, and the concept of vector species: these three lectures provide background and an overview of vector anatomy, physiology and genetics that prepares students for more in depth consideration of disease transmission and vector control during the course. They also introduce the idea of closely related species having very different capacity for disease transmission

Evolution of haematophagy, blood-sucking insects and host homeostasis: These series of lectures introduce the core concepts of blood feeding and disease transmission, including how blood feeding has evolved and how the host and vector have adapted to the invasive and risky process.

Host Choice and Location: This lecture series introduces the concepts of how vectors choose their hosts from the multiple blood sources available, and the behavioural responses invoked to find and feed on specific hosts.

Arbovirus, malaria, sleeping sickness, and filarial transmission: These series of lectures begin to dissect the role of specific vector species in the transmission of different major diseases transmitted by insects of medical importance, and the current research into the study of disease transmission.

Vector control: A series of three lectures then evaluates current methods of vector control through biological, chemical and environmental processes. Discussing how research is assisting with the development of improved approaches, including the design of entirely novel genetic tools.

Insecticide resistance: In line with the major research focus of the LSTM the formal taught course finishes with two lectures that discuss how insecticide resistance develops in vectors and the research taking place to overcome resistance.

Revision Tutorial: the course concludes with a revision tutorial, in which general feedback is given concerning the first coursework, and to re-emphasise the format of the assessment requirement . The lectures are supplemented by 1hr weekly interactive session provided synchronously.
Teaching and Learning Methods
Lecture Description: lectures are provided by research active staff (Liverpool Curriculum Framework - research connected teaching) that provide the core concepts to the topics in their specialist areas. Included in lectures are references to further reading material that expand on the topics to gain a fuller appreciation of the field through personal study.

Attendance Recorded: Yes

One interactive revision tutorial to the whole class is provided at the end of the course to give general feedback on coursework, and to prepare for final coursework submission exams. Individual feedback on coursework is provided through Canvas and by one to one meetings with markers that are requested by students.

Attendance Recorded: Yes

Notes: We elicit a mid-term review of the course from the students to respond and rectify any problems arising during course.

“Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.”
Assessment Strategy
Assessment 1 is a written coursework consisting of short answer questions. Assessment 2 consists of a written essay.

The assessments reflect real-life and career-based scenarios (Authentic assessment hallmark of the Liverpool Curriculum Framework).

Specifically, these assessments enhance digital fluency in our students, a skill highly sought after by graduate employers. Additionally, we are creating confident graduates who will excel in their field. These authentic assessments are research-connected and the skills for completion are taught through active learning.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.

This module is delivered by hybrid model of teaching and assessment and follows the Liverpool Curriculum Framework principles and attributes. Assessments are given as two open book written pieces.
Educational Aims
​To describe current research into vectors and vector-borne diseases

​To demonstrate how this research answers broad-ranging questions in vector biology and leads to novel vector control strategies
​​
​To develop knowledge and deep understanding in tropical disease biology, and ability to apply, critically evaluate and interpret this knowledge to solve complex problems
Marketing Module Synopsis
This module introduces students to current knowledge and research into insects and other vectors of disease. It has a major focus on vector biology, including life cycle, ecology, behaviour and methods of control. The vectors covered include different mosquito species, sandflies, tsetse flies and snails. The emphasis is on how understanding vector biology through theory and research is critical to the implementation of successful control programmes. There is also teaching on research into novel methods of control, focusing on malaria, dengue, sleeping sickness and schistosomiasis. The module is delivered by formal lectures and a revision tutorial, and is assessed by coursework.
Outline Syllabus
The course is comprised of 18 lectures and associated active learning sessions that introduce the field of vector biology and disease transmission with an emphasis on vector control. It is provided by lecturers that are all research active and give a perspective on how research into vector biology is used to inform disease control strategies.

The major topics that are covered in the course include:

Introduction to Insects and arthropods of medical importance, and the concept of vector species: these three lectures provide background and an overview of vector anatomy, physiology and genetics that prepares students for more in depth consideration of disease transmission and vector control during the course. They also introduce the idea of closely related species having very different capacity for disease transmission

Evolution of haematophagy, blood-sucking insects and host homeostasis: These series of lectures introduce the core concepts of blood feeding and disease transmission, including how blood feeding has evolved and how the host and vector have adapted to the invasive and risky process.

Host Choice and Location: This lecture series introduces the concepts of how vectors choose their hosts from the multiple blood sources available, and the behavioural responses invoked to find and feed on specific hosts.

Arbovirus, malaria, sleeping sickness, and filarial transmission: These series of lectures begin to dissect the role of specific vector species in the transmission of different major diseases transmitted by insects of medical importance, and the current research into the study of disease transmission.

Vector control: A series of three lectures then evaluates current methods of vector control through biological, chemical and environmental processes. Discussing how research is assisting with the development of improved approaches, including the design of entirely novel genetic tools.

Insecticide resistance: In line with the major research focus of the LSTM the formal taught course finishes with two lectures that discuss how insecticide resistance develops in vectors and the research taking place to overcome resistance.

Revision Tutorial: the course concludes with a revision tutorial, in which general feedback is given concerning the first coursework, and to re-emphasise the format of the assessment requirement . The lectures are supplemented by 1hr weekly interactive session provided synchronously.
Teaching and Learning Methods
Lecture Description: lectures are provided by research active staff (Liverpool Curriculum Framework - research connected teaching) that provide the core concepts to the topics in their specialist areas. Included in lectures are references to further reading material that expand on the topics to gain a fuller appreciation of the field through personal study.

Attendance Recorded: Yes

One interactive revision tutorial to the whole class is provided at the end of the course to give general feedback on coursework, and to prepare for final coursework submission exams. Individual feedback on coursework is provided through Canvas and by one to one meetings with markers that are requested by students.

Attendance Recorded: Yes

Notes: We elicit a mid-term review of the course from the students to respond and rectify any problems arising during course.

“Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for teaching may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.”
Assessment Strategy
Assessment 1 is a written coursework consisting of short answer questions. Assessment 2 consists of a written essay.

The assessments reflect real-life and career-based scenarios (Authentic assessment hallmark of the Liverpool Curriculum Framework).

Specifically, these assessments enhance digital fluency in our students, a skill highly sought after by graduate employers. Additionally, we are creating confident graduates who will excel in their field. These authentic assessments are research-connected and the skills for completion are taught through active learning.

Students will be informed that, in exceptional circumstances (e.g. global pandemics), plans for assessment may be subject to change. Contact will be made via the VLE and an e-mail will be sent to each of the students informing them of any changes that prove to be necessary. The Health and Life Sciences FAQSC will be informed in advance of any such changes, so that approval can be given.
